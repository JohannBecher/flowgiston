What is Quality of Identity?

Quality of Identity summarizes the approach we have taken to a peer-to-peer technology we call Agents.

Here you will find information about Agents—an application that allows individuals and other entities to have:

  • Fined grained control of access to information
  • Very strong anonymity and Very strong assurances of identity
  • Participation in a distributed and robust reputation system

Our Motivation

Information we create and own is being taken from us as the price to use online tools for connecting, communicating, and acquiring information online. This information is being sold back to us in the form of targeted advertising, security products, monitoring products, and access fees. Our effort is to offer an alternative that provides an infrastructure that is more secure than centralized solutions, tools that make managing quality of identity easy and intuitive, and an accessible and extensible programming model.

The Technology

This is a deep and rich technology and the information on this site is only an overview and is a work in progress. We make some seemingly outrageous claims and the final proof of those claims is in the code.

Principles of the Agent Technology

Peer-to-peer

Each Agent is self contained and can be directly accessed and controlled only by it’s owner. There is no dependence on a metasystem or directory, including IP addressing or tracker.

Authenticated connections

Any two Agents can share a secret (a unique identifier and, optionally an encryption mechanism) that allows each to identify communications from each other. That identifier is extremely difficult to discover and, when encryption of communications is added, nearly impossible to eavesdrop, intercept, or imitate.

Mutual connectivity

Any Agent can create a authenticated connection with another through the process of an Introduction. If either Agent deletes the connection, then neither Agent can communicate directly with the other.

Network independence

An Agent can be backed up and deleted on one machine and reinstalled on another machine in a different location and still be able to communicate to all Agents it was previously connected to, including messages that were on-route before relocating. Agents can communicate over multiple networks, as long as there is at least one accessible Agent spanning the networks.

Verifiable key properties

An Agent that is relying on another Agent to behave in a specific way has the ability to verify certain properties of the other Agent through model checking. For example, a provable property can include allowing forwarding of only specific information, such as a doctor viewing my medical records, but not being able to forward them to anyone else. Another provable property is the ability to correctly participate in a distributed transaction, like exchanges in the AbundX Exchange.

“Natural” storage and retrieval of information

Agents store and retrieve information via labels. Any piece of information can have any number of labels, including labels of labels. For example, a picture of my house may exist under Photos → Buildings → Picture-of-My-House and Houses → Home → Picture-of-My-House.

Fined-grained and easy to manage access to information

Allowing access to information is as simple as assigning a connection or group of connections to a label.

Robust and easy to use reputation system

We call the reputation system the Web of Provenance, since the quality of information is not stand-alone, or only part of a chain, but exists in an ecosystem. Agents make viewing and creating links in the Web of Provenance easy and rewarding.

FAQ about Agents

What is the approximate cost of hosting an Agent?

Our best estimate at this time is on the order of $20 to $50 per year with good security and redundancy. We will be offering an offshore, hardened, highly secure, premium hosting solution through a partner, but we encourage entrepreneurs and cooperatives to offer their own value-added hosting solutions.

Is this another anti-Facebook?

No! Facebook provides valuable services that Agents are not designed to replace. There will be some information and activities that are best hosted by FB and some that are more suitable to a secure P2P network that has assurances of control and quality. Where there is overlap, each individual can decide which provides the best value.

What do you mean when you say Agents shift the advantage from the attacker to the defender?

For every attack by an adversary, Agents have one or more effective defenses that are easier and cheaper to implement. In the current Internet, the attacker has the advantage. For example, if the private key is obtained through brute force to a public key encrypted network address, then all information at that address will be accessible. A much greater effort would be required to obtain only one piece of Agent managed information.

How do Agents support “qualities” of information?

Through Labels and the Web of Provenance. Any piece of data, for example the picture of a bicycle, can have labels, for example, “for sale”, “used”, “well maintained”, and “belongs to me”. Those labels have no use to anyone else, without some means to verify them. Any piece of Agent information can be supported in a Web of Provenance. For example, the local bicycle shop could provide evidence that I purchased the bicycle 2 years ago. Several dozen people can provide evidence that I have provided well maintained items in the past. The p2p exchange can show I have executed hundreds of transactions with no complaints from providers or receivers.

Isn’t providing evidence a cumbersome obligation?

First, it is purely optional. Second, providing evidence to support other Agent’s qualities typically is no more effort than assigning a label to a piece of information. From then on, all verifications can occur automatically.

Why do you have a video of an Agent and not provide a working demo?

Agents are single user applications and not built for multiple simultaneous users, so performance would rapidly deteriorate, if many people tried to use the prototype simultaneously.

How is quality of identity different from relationship?

A relationship can be thought of as the sum of the qualities of identity each person has with another plus expectations, emotions, and physical contact. Further, your qualities of identity can extend to others you may have no relationship with, or even know your identity. For example, your expert opinion supporting a fact is not strengthened by your name, but by your expertise, education, and experiences; someone who has no relationship to you may rely on that fact on the strength of qualities of your identity. By having tools to manage our identity, management of relationships follows easily and many more types and variations of relationships are possible. We can also gain control of who has access to our information and how it is used.

Technical Overview

Agents represent the next major step of evolution of the network that Van Jacobson envisioned as “Dissemination (focus on the data)”. This next step allows for a quality-based network that is no more complex than TCP/IP was when it was introduced and changed the definition of the network from path-based telephony making the Internet possible.

The qualities that the code of this new network of Agents enforce include strong authentication, granular control of data, verification of claims, strong anonymity, reliable distributed transactions, and communications that can be significantly more secure than what is feasible on the current Internet.

Our goal is to provide an alternative to existing cyber communication that can support information quality as part of the code, which is in turn based upon a mathematical model. The technology we developed is an application that can be implemented on any connected computer. The technology operates on the existing Internet, but is not be limited to it.

Specifically, an Agent will provide:

  • The ability to easily create persistent, mutual, secure, and verifiable Connections that are independent of any identity meta-system or central authority, including network addressing
  • The ability to easily and reliably control access to information
  • The ability to execute highly reliable peer-to-peer transactions

The underlying infrastructure is in the open source repository for SpecialK. We will be adding the Agent protocols and an individual Agent front-end application in the coming weeks. Additional technical documentation can be found there as well.

What is Quality of Identity?

Identity can be thought of as the observable or knowable information about a person. The veracity, relevance, and completeness of this information are integral parts of the qualities of identity. By this definition, each person’s quality of identity is different, if only slightly, with every other person he or she interacts with.

Some qualities of identity include:

  • Personally identifiable information (PII), such as your names and addresses
  • Profession, title, work history
  • Expertise, curriculum vitae
  • Education, grades, schools
  • Connections, such as friends, associates, acquaintances
  • Communications, content and with whom, was the information asked for or offered?
  • Activities and experiences
  • Artifacts created, such a pictures and music
  • Likes, dislikes, and other opinions
  • Medical condition and history
  • Others’ assessment, such as recommendations, endorsements

Identity vs Identification

Identity does not have to include personally identifiable information, for example when two people know each other by sight and not by name, or by an alias used online. Verified anonymity is where I can have assurances of certain qualities of identity of another, but not have the ability to attach that information to a specific person.

A key component to information verification is the ability to use reputation independent of identification. Verified anonymity allows an individual to have qualities of their identity supported by others without revealing personally identifiable information. This is very common in day-to-day interactions, but is virtually non-existent online. Every time you vote, or reference the source of information as a title or role (“a doctor told me”) you are using verified anonymity.

Why Quality of Identity is Important

The quality of information we encounter through various media and interactions is deteriorating rapidly. Quantity and volume have replaced quality and verification. In order to make better decisions, solve the right problems, and prioritize our efforts we need to have easy access to the qualities of the information we seek and receive.

All evidence of the qualities of information eventually comes from an individual or entity. The extent to which that evidence is accepted and acted upon is dependent upon the perceived qualities the receiver has for the provider of the evidence, in other words, the quality of identity.

This dynamic is taken for granted in everyday person-to-person interaction, but is poorly supported on the Internet.

If every claim we make that we want people to act upon is supportable by leveraging the web of quality of identities, then we increase our influence. If we can easily verify claims others make by having a view of the qualities of identities supporting those claims, we can make better decisions.

Agents and Quality of Identity

Our approach to managing online identity is to provide the ability to easily manage access to our information and easily participate in and leverage a web of provenance.

One approach to improving the quality of information is to make all information free and accessible. This may be possible, but in today’s world such an approach does not reward the creative only the compilers. And it creates an undue dependency on the compilers and undue influence through choosing what information and evidence is made most available.

An alternative to the two choices of centralized control of information and completely open and free information is distributed control of information: each creator of information has control over both who has access to their information and how it is used.

Web of Provenance

Provenance: place or source of origin.

The truthfulness or factualness of a piece of information can be supported by evidence, or the source(s) of the claim of truthfulness or factuality.

The Web of Provenance takes three forms: transparency, peer support (similar to a web of trust) and authorities.

Transparency

The ability to see activities of others can be accomplished in certain circumstances: All are willing participants, all have equal access to information, and, optionally, participants are anonymous, but verified by an authority (see below).

An example of transparency is the AbundX. All messages and transactions are visible to all participants. Thus it is possible to see the interactions and exchanges of everyone in an AbundX. Information in the exchange is extremely difficult to alter once it has been posted, so it is highly unlikely that someone has re-written history to suit themselves.

Peer Support

Any Agent can verify claims made by another Agent and optionally provide additional evidence. This capability has rich capabilities that include reliable verification through multiple degrees of separation and verified anonymity. We will be posting demonstrations to illustrate some of this potential in the near future.

Authority of Curated Support

An Agent that is specifically used to validate claims is called a Curator Service. Any Agent can act as a Curator Service, but specialized Agents can be used specifically for the act of curating evidence to support information presented by others. For example, a law office may verify residency and identity. The law firm would set up a Curator Service to provide verification for their clients.

Curator Services are designed to be hosted by third parties that have a vested interest in the veracity of the evidence they are presenting. The host of a Curator would typically have a published process they follow to validate evidence of a claim.

The Web of Provenance introduces three key innovations that avoid some significant problems of current trust architectures, can build upon their capabilities, and provide new capabilities that would be difficult to accomplish without a Web of Provenance.

The first key innovation of Curator Services is that they do not provide evidence of a claim without the permission of the claimant.

This ternary conversation is only possible if the claimant is consistently available to validate the legitimacy of the request to check the claim.

Because the Curator Service supporting a claim is a connection of the Agent, secure communication can be implemented between the claimant and the Curator Service to severely inhibit circumvention.

The second key innovation of Curator Services is the separation of the action of trust from the evidence. Depending upon the consequences of betrayal of trust, the recipient of a claim may need more evidence than one Curator Service provides. The recipient may want to explore the Web of Provenance around a claim before deciding to trust a claim. In the Web of Provenance, this can be accomplished with the click of a mouse.

The third key innovation of Curator Services is the separation of the source of the evidence from the evidence itself. This innovation creates three significant capabilities:

  1. It is possible to supply the evidence without revealing the source
  2. It is possible to supply the source of evidence without revealing the evidence
  3. It is possible to create “blinds” that separate the evidence from the source so that no one data source is able to link the two.
